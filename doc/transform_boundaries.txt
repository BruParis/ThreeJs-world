Transform boundaries are geologically interesting because they're primarily about lateral motion — crust is neither created nor destroyed, so they don't directly drive the same dramatic geological types as convergent or divergent boundaries. But they're not neutral either.
Geological types along transform boundaries
The dominant feature along transforms is pull-apart basins (also called rhomb grabens or transtensional basins). These form wherever the fault bends or steps — if the two plates move past each other and there's a releasing bend (the geometry opens up a gap), you get localized extension and subsidence. The Dead Sea, the Salton Sea trough, and the Sea of Marmara are classic examples. So you can assign basin geology in localized segments along transform boundaries, specifically where you introduce some geometric irregularity.
Conversely, a restraining bend (compression across the fault) creates a transpressional ridge — a small, elongated uplifted zone. This isn't full orogeny, but you could assign a lighter version of it, or a distinct type like a fold-and-thrust belt or just treat it as minor orogenic terrain. The Transverse Ranges in California form this way along the San Andreas.
In general, the crust flanking a long transform tends to be highly fractured and faulted — good territory for your shield or platform types being cut and disrupted rather than newly formed.
A practical rule: along transforms, assign basin in a probabilistic or geometry-triggered way (maybe 20–30% of transform boundary tiles get basin, favoring tiles where the local boundary direction shifts), and a smaller fraction get minor orogeny or fractured platform. Most tiles just inherit whatever their plate's background geology is.
Micro-plates and transform boundaries
Yes, this is a very natural connection. Micro-plates in reality tend to form in two main contexts, both involving transforms:
The first is at ridge-transform intersections. Where a mid-ocean ridge is offset by a long transform, the geometry can isolate a small block of crust that rotates independently — the Juan de Fuca plate, Easter microplate, and Rivera plate are examples of this. So if you have a divergent boundary that is offset (i.e., two divergent segments not perfectly aligned, connected by a transform), that connecting transform zone is a natural place to spawn a micro-plate. The micro-plate rotates because it's being pulled by spreading on both sides.
The second is at triple junctions — where three plates meet. These junctions are inherently unstable and tend to migrate or reorganize, and a micro-plate can stabilize the geometry. You could implement this by detecting tiles that are at the meeting point of three or more plate boundaries and flagging them as candidates for micro-plate nucleation.
Implementation sketch
For micro-plates, you could identify candidate regions as tiles that sit at or near the intersection of a transform with either a divergent boundary or another transform. You then "carve out" a small cluster of those tiles into their own plate with an independently derived motion vector — often something like the average of the two neighboring plate motions with an added rotational component. Their boundaries will then be re-evaluated and they'll likely end up with mixed convergent/divergent/transform boundary segments of their own.
This also gives your world more visual variety — a long featureless transform is geologically boring, but one that spawns a small rotating micro-plate with its own basin and ridge system feels much more alive.
